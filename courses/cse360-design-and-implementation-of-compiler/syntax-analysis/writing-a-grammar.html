# Writing a Grammar

This section begins with a discussion of how to divide work between a lexical analyzer and a parser. We then consider several transformations that could be applied to get a grammar more suitable for parsing. One technique can eliminate ambiguity in the grammar, and other techniques, left-recursion elimination and left factoring, are useful for rewriting grammars so they become suitable for top-down parsing.

# Lexical Versus Syntactic Analysis

We typically use regular expressions to construct lexical analyzers while using grammars to construct parsers. In fact, everything that can be described by a regular expression can also be described by a grammar. We may ask, why use regular expressions to define the lexical syntax. There are several reasons:

  1. The lexical rules of a language are frequently quite simple, and to describe them we do not need a notation as powerful as grammars.
  2. Regular expressions generally provide a more concise and easier-to-understand notation for tokens than grammars.

# Eliminating Ambiguity

Sometimes an ambiguous grammar can be rewritten to eliminate the ambiguity. As an example, we shall eliminate the ambiguity from the following "dangling-else" grammar:

stmt→ifexprthenstmt∣ifexprthenstmtelsestmt∣other\begin{matrix} \textit{stmt} & \rightarrow & \bold{if} \; \textit{expr} \; \bold{then} \; \textit{stmt} \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ & \vert & \bold{if} \; \textit{expr} \; \bold{then} \; \textit{stmt} \; \bold{else} \; \textit{stmt} \\ & \vert & \bold{other} \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ \end{matrix}stmt​→∣∣​ifexprthenstmtifexprthenstmtelsestmtother​
(1)

Here otherotherother stands for any other statement. According to this grammar, the compound conditional statement

ifE1thenifE2thenS1elseS2\bold{if} \; E_1 \; \bold{then} \; \bold{if} \; E_2 \; \bold{then} \; S_1 \; \bold{else} \; S_2ifE1​thenifE2​thenS1​elseS2​

is an example showing Grammar (1) is ambiguous since it has two parse trees

In all programming languages with conditional statements of this form, the first parse tree is preferred. The general rule is, Match each else\bold{else}else with the closest unmatched then\bold{then}then. This disambiguating rule can be incorporated directly into a grammar by using the following observations.

  • A statement appearing between a then\bold{then}then and a else\bold{else}else must be matched (if-then-else\bold{if}\text{-}\bold{then\text{-}else}if-then-else pairs).
  • Thus statements must split into kinds: matched\textit{matched}matched and unmatched\textit{unmatched}unmatched.
  • The unambiguous grammar for if-then-else\bold{if}\text{-}\bold{then\text{-}else}if-then-else statements can be described as
stmt→matched_stmt∣unmatched_stmtmatched_stmt→ifexprthenmatched_stmtelsematched_stmt∣otherunmatched_stmt→ifexprthenstmt∣ifexprthenmatched_stmtelseunmatched_stmt\begin{matrix} \textit{stmt} & \rightarrow & \textit{matched\_stmt} \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ & \vert & \textit{unmatched\_stmt} \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ \textit{matched\_stmt} & \rightarrow & \bold{if} \; \textit{expr} \; \bold{then} \; \textit{matched\_stmt} \; \bold{else} \; \textit{matched\_stmt} \;\;\; \\ & \vert & \bold{other} \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ \textit{unmatched\_stmt} & \rightarrow & \bold{if} \; \textit{expr} \; \bold{then} \; \textit{stmt} \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ & \vert & \bold{if} \; \textit{expr} \; \bold{then} \; \textit{matched\_stmt} \; \bold{else} \; \textit{unmatched\_stmt} \\ \end{matrix}stmtmatched_stmtunmatched_stmt​→∣→∣→∣​matched_stmtunmatched_stmtifexprthenmatched_stmtelsematched_stmtotherifexprthenstmtifexprthenmatched_stmtelseunmatched_stmt​

# Elimination of Left Recursion

A grammar is left recursive if it has a nonterminal AAA such that there is a derivation A⇒+AαA \xRightarrow{+} A \alphaA+​Aα for some string α\alphaα. Top-down parsing methods cannot handle left-recursive grammars, so a transformation is needed to eliminate left recursion.

# A simple left recursion

The left-recursive production A→Aα∣βA \rightarrow A \alpha \vert \betaA→Aα∣β could be replaced by non-left-recursive productions without changing the strings derivable from AAA.

A→βA′A′→αA′∣ϵ\begin{matrix} A & \rightarrow & \beta A' \;\; \\ A' & \rightarrow & \alpha A' \; \vert \; \epsilon \\ \end{matrix}AA′​→→​βA′αA′∣ϵ​

E→E+T∣TT→T∗F∣FF→(E)∣id\begin{matrix} E & \rightarrow & E + T \; \vert \; T \\ T & \rightarrow & T * F \; \vert \; F \\ F & \rightarrow & \lparen E \rparen \; \vert \; \bold{id} \;\;\; \\ \end{matrix}ETF​→→→​E+T∣TT∗F∣F(E)∣id​

E→TE′E′→+TE′∣ϵ\begin{matrix} E & \rightarrow & TE' \;\;\;\;\;\; \\ E' & \rightarrow & +TE' \; \vert \; \epsilon \\ \end{matrix}EE′​→→​TE′+TE′∣ϵ​

T→FT′T′→∗FT′∣ϵ\begin{matrix} T & \rightarrow & FT' \;\;\;\;\;\; \\ T' & \rightarrow & *FT' \; \vert \; \epsilon \\ \end{matrix}TT′​→→​FT′∗FT′∣ϵ​

# The general case

Here, we shall deduce the general form for this formula. Immediate left recursion can be eliminated by the following technique, which works for any number of AAA-productions. First, group the productions as

A→Aα1∣Aα2∣⋯∣Aαm∣β1∣β2∣⋯∣βnA \rightarrow A \alpha_1 \; \vert \; A \alpha_2 \; \vert \; \cdots \; \vert \; A \alpha_m \; \vert \; \beta_1 \; \vert \; \beta_2 \; \vert \; \cdots \; \vert \; \beta_nA→Aα1​∣Aα2​∣⋯∣Aαm​∣β1​∣β2​∣⋯∣βn​

where no βi\beta_iβi​ begins with an AAA. Then replace the AAA-productions by

A→β1A′∣β2A′∣⋯∣βnA′A′→α1A′∣α2A′∣⋯∣αmA′∣ϵ\begin{matrix} A & \rightarrow & \beta_1 A' \; \vert \; \beta_2 A' \; \vert \; \cdots \; \vert \; \beta_n A' \;\;\;\;\;\; \\ A' & \rightarrow & \alpha_1 A' \; \vert \; \alpha_2 A' \; \vert \; \cdots \; \vert \; \alpha_m A' \; \vert \; \epsilon \\ \end{matrix}AA′​→→​β1​A′∣β2​A′∣⋯∣βn​A′α1​A′∣α2​A′∣⋯∣αm​A′∣ϵ​

This eliminates all immediate left recursion, but it doesn't eliminate left recursion involving derivations of two ore more steps (not immediate). For example

S→Aa∣bA→Ac∣Sd∣ϵ\begin{matrix} S & \rightarrow & Aa \; \vert \; b \;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ A & \rightarrow & Ac \; \vert \; Sd \; \vert \; \epsilon \\ \end{matrix}SA​→→​Aa∣bAc∣Sd∣ϵ​
(2)

The nonterminal SSS is left recursive because S⇒Aa⇒SdaS \Rightarrow Aa \Rightarrow SdaS⇒Aa⇒Sda but it is not immediately left recursive. Hence it can not be eliminate by the above technique. But still, in some cases we can try to handle it by the substitution. The second production in the grammar (2) becomes

A→Ac∣Aad∣bd∣ϵA \rightarrow Ac \; \vert \; Aad \; \vert \; bd \; \vert \; \epsilonA→Ac∣Aad∣bd∣ϵ

Eliminating the immediate left recursion among these AAA-productions yields the following grammar.

S→Aa∣bA→bdA′∣A′A′→cA′∣adA′∣ϵ\begin{matrix} S & \rightarrow & Aa \; \vert \; b \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ A & \rightarrow & bdA' \; \vert \; A' \;\;\;\;\;\; \\ A' & \rightarrow & cA' \; \vert \; adA' \; \vert \; \epsilon \\ \end{matrix}SAA′​→→→​Aa∣bbdA′∣A′cA′∣adA′∣ϵ​

# Left Factoring

Left factoring is a grammar transformation that is useful for producing a grammar suitable for predictive, or top-down, parsing. When the choice between two alternative AAA-productions is not clear, we may be able to rewrite the productions to defer the decision until enough of the input has been seen that we can make the right choice.

For example, if we have the two productions

stmt→ifexprthenstmt∣ifexprthenstmtelsestmt∣other\begin{matrix} \textit{stmt} & \rightarrow & \bold{if} \; \textit{expr} \; \bold{then} \; \textit{stmt} \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ & \vert & \bold{if} \; \textit{expr} \; \bold{then} \; \textit{stmt} \; \bold{else} \; \textit{stmt} \\ & \vert & \bold{other} \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ \end{matrix}stmt​→∣∣​ifexprthenstmtifexprthenstmtelsestmtother​
(1)

on seeing the input token if\bold{if}if, we cannot immediately tell which production to choose to expand stmt\textit{stmt}stmt. In general, suppose a grammar A→αβ1∣αβ2∣⋯∣αβn∣γA \rightarrow \alpha \beta_1 \; \vert \; \alpha \beta_2 \; \vert \; \cdots \; \vert \; \alpha \beta_n \; \vert \; \gammaA→αβ1​∣αβ2​∣⋯∣αβn​∣γ where γ\gammaγ represents all alternatives that do not begin with α\alphaα, and the input begin with a nonempty string derived from α\alphaα, we do not know which production to use.

However, we may defer the decision by changing the grammar into

A→αA′∣γA′→β1∣β2∣⋯∣βn\begin{matrix} A & \rightarrow & \alpha A' \; \vert \; \gamma \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \\ A' & \rightarrow & \beta_1 \; \vert \; \beta_2 \; \vert \; \cdots \; \vert \; \beta_n \\ \end{matrix}AA′​→→​αA′∣γβ1​∣β2​∣⋯∣βn​​

Here A′A'A′ is a new nonterminal. Repeatedly apply this transformation until no two alternatives for a nonterminal have a common prefix.
